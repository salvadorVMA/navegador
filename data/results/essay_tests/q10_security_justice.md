# Analytical Essay

**Query:** ¿Qué relación ven los mexicanos entre seguridad pública y justicia?

## Summary
The relationship between public security and justice perceptions among Mexicans is generally weak, with most bivariate associations showing low effect sizes and non-significant p-values. However, moderate and significant associations exist between current security perceptions and reasons for obeying laws, as well as between future security expectations and economic justice perceptions, indicating some linkage between how justice is perceived and feelings about security. Overall, evidence quality is moderate with nine variable pairs analyzed, a few showing significant but modest associations, suggesting cautious confidence in these findings.

## Data Landscape
Nine variables related to public security and justice perceptions were analyzed, drawn from surveys measuring personal and national security views, economic and political justice perceptions, attitudes toward justice practices, and motivations for law obedience. The distributions are highly fragmented: two variables show polarization, two lean toward a dominant view, and five are dispersed, reflecting a lack of consensus in public opinion. The divergence index confirms complete non-consensus across all variables, indicating deep fragmentation in how Mexicans perceive security and justice.

## Evidence
Cross-tabulations reveal mostly weak relationships between security and justice perceptions. For example, perceptions of personal security compared to a year ago (p3|SEG) remain similar regardless of views on economic justice (p1|JUS), with the proportion feeling "Un poco más inseguro" ranging narrowly from 14.3% to 26.5% across economic justice categories (V=0.038, p=0.756). Similarly, personal security perceptions vary little by political justice views (p2|JUS), with "Igual (esp.)" security responses between 21.4% and 53.6% but no significant association (V=0.062, p=0.341). Attitudes toward torture for information (p4|JUS) also show no significant link to security feelings (V=0.057, p=0.164). However, reasons for obeying laws (p7|JUS) significantly relate to current security perceptions (p3|SEG) (V=0.103, p=0.000). For instance, those obeying laws "Para evitar daños a mi familia y amistades" report feeling "Igual (esp.)" secure at 47.1%, while those citing "Otra" reasons report only 14.3%. Future security expectations (p4|SEG) also vary significantly with economic justice views (p1|JUS) (V=0.118, p=0.000), where expectations of "Mucho mejor" security range from 2.4% among those seeing economic justice as "Igual de bien (esp.)" to 40.9% among those with no opinion (NS). Political justice perceptions (p2|JUS) weakly but significantly influence future security expectations (V=0.079, p=0.003), with "Igual" security expectations ranging from 34.1% to 57.0%. Demographically, region is the strongest moderator, with moderate effect sizes (mean V=0.14) influencing security and justice opinions, such as more negative security perceptions in region 02. Women and younger age groups show slightly different patterns, but these are weaker (V=0.10 and 0.09 respectively). Univariate distributions confirm fragmented opinions: for example, 31.2% feel their personal security is "Igual (esp.)" compared to last year, with 27.4% feeling "Un poco más seguro" and 20.4% "Un poco más inseguro." Justice perceptions are polarized, with 37.9% saying the economic situation is "Peor" and 37.3% "Igual de mal (esp.)."

## Complications
Demographically, region is the strongest moderator of opinions, with moderate effect sizes (mean V=0.14) influencing both security and justice views. For instance, region 02 reports 29% feeling "Igual (esp.)" secure versus 39% feeling "Mucho más inseguro," indicating regional disparities in security perception. Gender and age have weaker moderating effects (V=0.10 and 0.09 respectively), with women slightly more likely to report longer neighborhood residence and younger respondents more likely to obey laws for moral reasons. Minority opinions are substantial and important: about 19.2% agree with torture for information, and 19.8% describe the political situation as "Peligrosa," reflecting significant dissenting views on justice and security. The simulation-based bivariate associations rely on SES bridge assumptions and may have limited precision, particularly given the modest effect sizes and fragmented opinion distributions. Several expected relationships, such as between economic or political justice perceptions and current personal security, are weak or absent (p > 0.05, V < 0.1), underscoring the complexity and possible independence of these constructs in public perception. These factors complicate drawing strong causal inferences or policy prescriptions from the data.

## Implications
First, the moderate and significant association between reasons for obeying laws and current security perceptions suggests that promoting a shared social understanding of law compliance as benefiting all could enhance feelings of security. Policies fostering civic education and communal responsibility may strengthen this link. Second, the significant relationship between economic justice perceptions and future security expectations implies that improving public trust in economic fairness and justice institutions could positively influence security outlooks. Thus, reforms aimed at economic justice and transparency may indirectly bolster public security confidence. However, the weak or absent links between many justice and security measures caution against simplistic assumptions that improving justice perceptions alone will directly enhance security feelings. Policymakers should consider multifaceted approaches addressing both justice system credibility and localized security conditions. Finally, the demographic disparities and fragmented opinions highlight the need for regionally tailored interventions and inclusive dialogues to address diverse public concerns about justice and security.
