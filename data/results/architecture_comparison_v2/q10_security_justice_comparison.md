Mexicans predominantly perceive a strong deficit in justice within public security institutions, with 74.4% believing the police do not resolve problems fairly (p25a_1|JUS), and 40.2% asserting that the Federal Police violate human rights (p29_4|SEG). ## Introduction
This analysis examines ten variables related to public security and justice perceptions among Mexicans, revealing a highly fragmented and polarized landscape. Nine out of ten variables show non-consensus distributions, with one consensus variable, two leaning variables, one polarized, and six dispersed, indicating widespread divergence in public opinion. The data probe trust in security institutions, attitudes toward extrajudicial actions, and perceptions of justice effectiveness, setting up a dialectical tension between dominant distrust and significant dissent regarding the security-justice nexus.

## Prevailing View
The dominant pattern is a profound skepticism toward the justice dimension of public security institutions. A strong consensus emerges in the belief that the police do not resolve problems fairly, with 74.4% responding "No" to whether police act justly (p25a_1|JUS). Additionally, 40.2% affirm that the Federal Police violate human rights (p29_4|SEG), the modal response with a 14.8 percentage point lead over the next option. Regarding personal security perceptions, 41.5% feel "Algo seguro" (somewhat safe) in their locality (p8_1|SEG), and 30.4% feel similarly at the state level (p8_3|SEG), suggesting a modest sense of security coexists with justice concerns. These lean and consensus responses indicate that most Mexicans link public security with significant justice deficits and human rights violations, fostering distrust in formal institutions.

## Counterargument
Despite dominant distrust, the data reveal substantial fragmentation and polarization that complicate a simple interpretation. Opinions on extrajudicial killings by security forces are dispersed: 35.0% are "En desacuerdo" (disagree), 24.6% are neutral, and 17.6% agree with such killings (p65|SEG), showing moral and legal tensions. Community lynching also divides opinion, with 31.8% disagreeing, 27.2% neutral, and 17.2% agreeing (p69|SEG). The most polarized variable concerns community rights to punish when authorities fail: 43.6% say communities "No tienen el derecho" (do not have the right), but 31.2% say they "Sí tienen el derecho, en parte" (have the right in part), and 17.1% affirm full right (p68|SEG), indicating a sharp split on vigilante justice legitimacy. Furthermore, 25.5% deny human rights violations by the Federal Police (p29_4|SEG), and 19.1% believe police do act justly (p25a_1|JUS), representing significant minority dissent. Trust in justice institutions like the Public Ministry and Municipal Police is highly fragmented with no dominant views (p54_16|SEG, p54_5|SEG), and perceptions of police impartiality are similarly dispersed (p62b_4|SEG). These divisions highlight that while distrust is widespread, a substantial portion of the population either supports or is ambivalent about extrajudicial and community justice measures, reflecting deep societal cleavages on the security-justice relationship.

## Implications
First, policymakers emphasizing the prevailing view might prioritize institutional reforms to enhance police accountability, human rights respect, and justice system fairness, aiming to rebuild trust in formal security and justice institutions. This approach assumes that restoring legitimacy will reduce support for extrajudicial and vigilante actions. Second, those focusing on the counterargument might recognize the entrenched polarization and fragmented opinions as signs that formal justice reforms alone may not suffice; they might consider integrating community-based security initiatives and dialogue mechanisms that address local demands for justice, including regulated forms of community enforcement, to bridge the gap between formal institutions and public expectations. The high polarization warns against relying solely on majority opinions for policy design, as significant minorities hold divergent views that could fuel social conflict if unaddressed.

# Analytical Essay (v2 — Thematic)

**Query:** ¿Qué relación ven los mexicanos entre seguridad pública y justicia?

## Overview
Mexicans perceive a strong link between public security and justice, characterized by widespread distrust in law enforcement's fairness and respect for human rights, alongside ambivalent feelings about personal safety. While many reject extrajudicial violence, significant divisions exist regarding the use of force and community-administered justice, reflecting complex and fragmented public attitudes. The following sections explore perceptions of police conduct, feelings of safety, and attitudes toward force and vigilante justice to illuminate this multifaceted relationship.

## Perceptions of Police Conduct and Trust in Law Enforcement
Mexicans generally view law enforcement as violating human rights and failing to administer justice fairly, indicating pervasive skepticism toward security institutions. For example, 40.2% believe the Policía Federal violates human rights (p29_4|SEG), and an overwhelming 74.4% consider that the police do not resolve problems justly (p25a_1|JUS). Trust in municipal police and the Ministerio Público is also low, with no strong consensus on confidence levels (p54_5|SEG, p54_16|SEG), underscoring widespread doubts about the impartiality and effectiveness of these authorities.

## Perceptions of Personal and Community Safety
Opinions on safety in local communities and states are mixed but tend to lean toward moderate feelings of security. The largest group feels 'somewhat safe' in their locality (41.5%, p8_1|SEG) and state (30.4%, p8_3|SEG), yet a substantial proportion expresses uncertainty or insecurity, reflecting diverse experiences with crime. This dispersion suggests that while many Mexicans do not feel entirely unsafe, perceptions of security vary considerably across different contexts and populations.

## Attitudes Toward Use of Force and Vigilante Justice
There is notable division regarding the justification of lethal force by security forces and community-led punishment of criminals, revealing tensions between demands for justice and concerns about legality and human rights. Although the largest single group disagrees with security forces killing organized crime members even if capture is possible (35.0%, p65|SEG), a significant minority supports or is neutral toward such force. Similarly, 43.6% reject community members' right to take justice into their own hands when authorities fail (p68|SEG), but 48.3% partially or fully support it, indicating polarized views. Opinions on lynching criminals are also fragmented, with 31.8% disagreeing and 26.6% agreeing or neutral (p69|SEG). This polarization highlights the complexity of public attitudes toward extrajudicial responses to crime.

## Synthesis
The data reveal that Mexicans link public security and justice through a lens of deep distrust in law enforcement's fairness and human rights adherence, coupled with ambivalent perceptions of personal safety. The strong consensus on police unfairness contrasts with the fragmented and polarized views on the use of force and community justice, suggesting that while many reject extrajudicial violence, a substantial portion sympathizes with or tolerates it as a response to perceived institutional failures. For policymakers and researchers, this underscores the critical need to rebuild trust in formal justice institutions and address public concerns about security without resorting to human rights violations or encouraging vigilantism. The main challenge lies in the fragmented nature of public opinion, which complicates the design of policies that must balance demands for effective crime control with respect for legal norms and human rights protections.
